Self-Fulfilling Prophecies in Financial Markets

Markets are driven by human psychology and perception which creates the existence of self-fulfilling prophecies.
Written by
Samson Qian
Epistemology
November 1, 2024
5 min read

Self-fulfilling prophecies are events where merely one's belief and perception of something influence their behavior to make the belief come true. A simple example of a self-fulfilling prophecy could be how a student who believes they will fail an exam and, as a result, studies less, ends up actually failing. The student's belief influenced their behavior and decision-making to contribute to the belief. Similarly, a student who believes they will succeed and, as a result, studies more, ends up actually succeeding. As financial markets are the reflections of investors' beliefs and perceptions, self-fulfilling prophecies are very prominent.

Momentum is an example of a self-fulfilling prophecy in financial markets. When investors believe a stock will rise, they buy it, driving the price up and confirming their belief. Conversely, if they believe a stock will fall, they sell it, driving the price down and confirming that belief. This creates a feedback loop where beliefs influence actions, which in turn reinforce those beliefs. At the micro level, many quant strategies will use similar alpha signals that capture similar properties in asset prices.
